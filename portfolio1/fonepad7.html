Asus Fonepad 7 is the research and strategy portion of a 7" android tablet phone I designed for Asus

problem definition

User experience of 7 inch tablet phones is awkward in many situations. Our aim is to improve and streamline the UX of Asus’ next generation 7 inch phone. From left to right:

  1. Difficult to talk and looks awkward
  2. Difficult to get in and out of pockets
  3. Difficult to use with one hand

project goals

  1. Conduct a usability test to compare the user experience between 4.5” & 7” tablet phones in various tasks and environments
  2. Define our target audience and their needs
  3. Select the most worthy solving problems and brainstorm design solutions to improve the user experience

PROJECT TYPE

Professional project

My ROLE

Usability testing, design strategy

ORGANIZATION

Asus in-house teams

RESEARCH SUMMARY

Our target users are people who rarely talk on the phone and use their phones primarily for text messaging and internet browsing. Someone who talks on the phone for hours everyday wouldn’t buy this phone. Potential buyers are very price conscious and care a lot about CP value (cost vs. performance). Single hand operation is also an important factor in their buying decisions. Below are key insights from the interview data:

Bang for the Buck
”I’m a very practical person. High C/P value is my priority. When it comes to appearance, I chose the one that is durable and good looking”
Low Phone Usage
“My communication with my friends are pretty much entirely over text messages (free using Google voice), email, and Google Talk”
Single Hand Operation
“After using Samsung Note 2, I started to miss smaller phones. One hand control is really important”

size vs mobility

As shown below, 7 inch phone is a crossover product between a smartphone and a large tablet. Mobility decreases drastically when the size exceeds 5.5 inch. Men like smaller phones because phones larger than 6” are hard to get in and out of pockets. Women on the other hand don’t mind 6.5” phones because they put their phones inside their purses. While the Asian and European market prefer 6” phones, the American market likes 6.5” phones better.

usability testing

We converted our smartphones (ranging from 4-5 inches) into 7 inch tablet phones with rough mock ups. Some of us stapled paper cut outs around our phones; some of us double sided taped our phones onto rear cases of 7 inch tablets. We performed a series of tasks at various locations with both smaller smartphones and 7 inch tablet phones. We analyzed and compared the user experience of both phones for each task at the given location. We assigned a score to every task performed by the phones at each location and averaged our scores. Some tasks have significant score differences while others have little to no differences.

design opportunities

The diagram below shows activities with the greatest score differences. We feel they are design opportunities. In the office, activities the greatest score difference are web browsing, text messaging, and calling. At home, activities with the greatest score differences are text messaging, calling, and online shopping. In public transportation, activities with the greatest score differences are cycling, note taking, calling, and reading.
